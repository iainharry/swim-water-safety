Purpose of this sheet: Your Year 9–10 unit covers sophisticated theory — drowning physiology, CPR mechanics, social determinants, and prevention evidence. This sheet helps you connect that theory to what you actually do in the pool. For each practical skill, you will identify which workbook concept it demonstrates, rate your own performance, and reflect on what the theory tells you about how to improve.
1 — Theory-to-Practice Connections
Practical Skill Theory Connection (Workbook Section) How the theory explains WHY this skill matters What good performance looks like
Survival float (face-down float with head turn to breathe) Section 2 — Physiology; 1-10-1 Cold Water Rule Floating conserves energy during the critical 10-minute swimming window. Each unnecessary movement depletes the window faster. Relaxed body position; minimal movement; controlled head turn; sustainable for 5+ minutes
Treading water — 3 minutes Section 2 — Cardiac output, energy conservation Treading uses significantly more energy than floating. The physiological case for floating is grounded in energy conservation during hypoxic threat. Egg-beater or scissor kick; arms sculling; minimal forward movement; controlled breathing throughout
Clothed float and swim Section 2 — Cold water physiology; 1-10-1 Rule Most drowning incidents happen in everyday clothing. Clothing changes buoyancy and drag. RLSS UK "Float to Live" specifically addresses this scenario. Able to maintain float with clothes on; adjusts swimming technique for added resistance
50-metre distance swim Section 2 — Cardiovascular fitness and drowning risk Swimming ability is a protective factor but is NOT the primary cause of adult drowning — most Victorian drowning victims could swim to some degree. Completes 50m; uses an efficient stroke; paced rather than sprinted
Throw rescue — rope and ring Section 1 (Y7-8 consolidation); RLSSA throw hierarchy "Throw, Don't Go" — entering water to rescue an untrained person risks a double drowning. Throw rescue is the safest intervention for an untrained bystander. Accurate throw reaching the victim; clear verbal instruction; able to explain RLSSA hierarchy
CPR — correct compression rate and depth Section 2 — CPR cardiac output; 4-minute window CPR at 100–120 bpm and 5–6cm depth generates ~25–30% of normal cardiac output — enough to sustain brain perfusion. Too slow or too shallow produces inadequate output. 100–120 bpm (metronome test); 5–6cm depth; full chest recoil; 30:2 ratio maintained; continues without stopping
Distress signal — raise one arm Section 1 (Y7-8); SwimSafe Rule 5 One raised arm is the internationally recognised swimmer distress signal. Waving both arms looks like play; a single raised arm signals emergency. Practised in water; one arm clearly extended vertically; able to maintain float while signalling
Cold water entry — feet first, step-in Section 2 — Cold water shock, gasping reflex Jumping into cold water triggers cold water shock — involuntary gasping that can cause immediate aspiration. Feet-first step-in gives the body time to adjust. Enters feet first; pauses at waist depth; controls breathing before proceeding; articulates the physiological reason

3 — Theory-Informed Reflection

Which practical skill was most difficult for you? Use your knowledge of physiology from Workbook Section 2 to explain WHY that skill is physically demanding — not just that it was hard.

e.g. If CPR was difficult, explain in terms of cardiac output requirements, compression rate physiology, and why depth matters.

The data from Workbook Section 1 shows that most Victorian adult drowning victims could swim. How does your practical experience change or deepen your understanding of why knowing how to swim is NOT the same as being water safe at a Year 9–10 level?

The RLSS UK "Float to Live" campaign argues that floating should come BEFORE attempting to swim in a water emergency. After your practical sessions, do you agree with this advice? Use specific evidence from your own experience in the pool and from the theory.

I agree / partially agree / disagree (circle one)

Identify ONE connection between a social determinant you studied (Workbook Section 3) and a practical skill gap you observed — either in yourself or in the class generally. How does this connection explain one aspect of Victorian drowning risk?

e.g. A student who did not grow up swimming may struggle with the survival float because they never had access to lessons — this is a structural access gap, not a personal failing.
