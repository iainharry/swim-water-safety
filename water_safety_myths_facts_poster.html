MYTHS vs FACTS

Diving & Shallow Water — What You Think You Know Could Kill You

The most common cause of aquatic spinal injury is diving or jumping into water that was shallower than expected. The rule is simple: FEET FIRST — FIRST TIME. EVERY TIME. Never dive or jump into water you haven't checked depth on — right now, today.

Myth

"If other people are jumping there, it must be safe."

Fact

Water depth changes daily due to tides, drought, floods, and sandbank movement. Someone who jumped safely yesterday could hit bottom today. The Bawley Beach sandbank case (NSW 2024) is a real example.

Myth

"Clear water means deep water."

Fact

Clarity tells you nothing about depth. Dangerously shallow water can be crystal clear. Rivers after dry periods and beach channels near sandbanks are classic examples of clear but fatally shallow water.

Myth

"I've jumped here before — I know this spot."

Fact

Prior experience creates false confidence. Rivers, beaches, and dams constantly change shape due to sediment, tides, drought, and flooding. The Bawley Beach case: a sandbank formed between visits. Always check. Every time.

Myth

"Only bad swimmers get hurt diving."

Fact

Strong swimmers suffer the same spinal injuries. A cervical spine fracture happens on impact — before swimming ability is relevant. In the Australian review, most patients were healthy young males. 66% were permanently disabled.

Myth

"Jumping feet-first is safe — you won't hit your head."

Fact

Jumping from height into shallow water can still cause broken legs, fractured pelvis, compressed spine, head trauma, and drowning. Feet-first impact at speed into hard bottom is not safe — it just changes which bones break.

Myth

"The jetty is high, so the water must be deep."

Fact

Jetty height tells you nothing about water depth. Many jetties extend over tidal shallows or sandbanks. The Terrigal Wharf case (NSW 2025): a teenage boy suffered serious spinal injuries — the height of the structure above was completely unrelated to the depth below.

Myth

"Diving injuries only happen in pools."

Fact

The majority of serious diving injuries occur in rivers, beaches, dams, lakes, and off jetties — not pools. Royal Life Saving Australia: more people drown in inland waterways than any other location in Australia.

Myth

"If you hit the bottom you'll just bounce up."

Fact

Cervical spine compression causes permanent paralysis instantly. There is no "bounce." The vertebrae in your neck fracture or dislocate on impact, severing or compressing the spinal cord. Two-thirds of Australian patients have permanent disability.

Myth

"Diving injuries are rare — it won't happen to me."

Fact

Unsafe diving accounts for 10% of all spinal cord injuries and 20% of quadriplegia cases in Australia. The highest incidence is in children aged 10–14 — the age group reading this poster. It happens most to people who think it won't.

Myth

"The dam / river looks the same as last summer."

Fact

Inland waterways change with every season, every rainfall event, and every drought. Depth, submerged objects, currents, and water temperature can all be dramatically different from one visit to the next — even one week to the next.

The 5 Rules — Every Time, Every Location

1 Always enter feet first in unknown water — no exceptions
2 Check depth right now, today — not based on last time
3 Never dive from wharves, jetties, bridges, rocks, or banks
4 Treat all inland waterways (rivers, dams, lakes) as unpredictable
5 If pressured to jump — the answer is always no. No explanation needed.
