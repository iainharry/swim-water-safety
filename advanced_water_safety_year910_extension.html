Advanced Data Analysis — Beyond the Standard Workbook

Q1.1 — Statistical Modelling 5 marks

Using the Victorian drowning data from LSV (lsv.com.au/research), calculate drowning rates per 100,000 for THREE consecutive years. Plot these on a hand-drawn or digital line graph. Then apply a linear trend line and extrapolate: what would the predicted 2026–27 rate be if the trend continues? What are the limitations of this prediction?

Show all calculations. The trend line must be justified — not guessed. Discuss at least two factors that could cause actual figures to deviate significantly from your prediction.

Q1.2 — Disaggregation Challenge 4 marks

The LSV reports an overall Victorian drowning rate of 0.78 per 100,000. However, this single figure obscures enormous demographic variation. Calculate (or estimate using LSV data) the drowning rate specifically for: (a) Victorian males, and (b) CALD Victorians. Compare these to the overall rate and explain what "disaggregating the data" reveals that aggregate statistics hide.

For CALD rate: use 40% of deaths and 28% of population. For male rate: 46 of 54 deaths, approx 50% of the population. Show your working.

Physiology — Advanced Applications

Q2.1 — Physiological Mechanism Analysis 4 marks

The 1-10-1 cold water rule states approximately 10 minutes of useful swimming ability is available after cold water immersion. Explain the specific physiological mechanism that LIMITS swimming ability during this window — not simply that "the body gets cold." Your answer must include: peripheral vasoconstriction, neuromuscular dysfunction, and the specific reason why fitness level does NOT significantly extend this window.

Cite in full.

Structural Analysis — Advanced Social Determinants

Q3.1 — Intersectionality Application 5 marks

The concept of intersectionality explains that individuals experience compounded disadvantage when multiple social identities overlap. Apply this framework to Victorian drowning risk. Identify a specific profile of a person who faces MULTIPLE intersecting risk factors — and explain how each factor amplifies the others rather than simply adding to them. Your response must reference at least two primary data sources.

Example profile to consider: a recently arrived CALD male, aged 19, in a regional area who fishes commercially. How do the risk factors interact and amplify each other — not just accumulate?

Victorian pool fencing legislation mandates four-sided isolation fencing for new residential pools. Research the specific legislative requirements (Consumer Affairs Victoria or local council regulations). Identify ONE significant gap or limitation in current pool fencing legislation — and construct an argument for a specific, practical amendment that would address it. Cite the legislation.

Q3.3 — Health Equity Analysis 5 marks

Distinguish between health equality and health equity. Then argue: should a Victorian Government water safety prevention budget be distributed equally across all population groups, or equitably (with more resources directed to higher-risk groups)? Construct a fully justified argument using Victorian drowning data as your primary evidence. Acknowledge and refute the strongest counter-argument to your position.

Prevention Strategy — Advanced Evaluation

Q4.1 — Evidence Quality Assessment 4 marks

Locate the specific research evidence underpinning the claim that "four-sided pool isolation fencing reduces child drowning by approximately 83%." Evaluate: (a) what type of study produced this figure, (b) where this study was conducted and how transferable its findings are to contemporary Victoria, and (c) what would be needed to update this evidence for current Victorian conditions. Cite your source.

Q4.2 — Cost-Effectiveness Analysis 5 marks

A concept used in public health is cost per life saved — how much investment produces one prevented death. Research (or estimate from available data) the approximate cost of: (a) extending patrolled beach hours by 20% across Victoria, (b) funding CALD community swim programs reaching 10,000 participants per year. For each, estimate the number of drowning deaths that might be prevented annually. Which provides better cost-effectiveness, and what are the significant limitations of this type of analysis?

This requires synthesis and estimation — there is no single correct number. Your reasoning and evidence quality matter more than the specific figures you arrive at.

Q6.2 — Intersectionality: Compound Risk Analysis 5 marks

The data shows 95% of aquatic spinal injury patients are male, the highest incidence group is 10–14 year olds, and inland waterways (often in rural or remote areas) account for the majority of serious incidents. Apply the intersectionality framework to construct a compound risk profile. Identify a specific type of person who faces intersecting, mutually amplifying risk factors — and argue why addressing any single factor in isolation will be insufficient. Your argument must reference at least two statistics and acknowledge one counter-argument.

Intersectionality means the risk factors multiply each other — they do not simply add. A rural teenage male faces compounded risk from masculinity norms + limited swim access + no lifeguard infrastructure + alcohol availability + delayed emergency response. Explain the amplification mechanisms.
Q6.3 — False Confidence as a Structural Problem 4 marks

The three Australian case studies (Terrigal Wharf NSW 2025, Torquay Front Beach Vic 2025, Bawley Beach NSW 2024) all involve a version of false confidence — either "it looked safe" or "I've done it before." Argue that false confidence near water is NOT primarily an individual attitude problem but a structural communications failure. What should water safety organisations, local councils, and schools be doing differently — and why are current approaches insufficient? Cite at least one primary source.

Think about: the difference between warning signs (which address knowledge) and behaviour change infrastructure (which addresses the decision-making environment). What would a structural solution look like versus an educational one?

Summative Assessment — Extension Prompts

Q6.1 — Advanced Assessment Task Options Extension

Choose ONE of the following extension versions of the summative assessment task:

Option A★ — Senate Submission (800–1,000 words)
Write a formal submission to a hypothetical Senate inquiry into drowning prevention funding in Australia. Argue for a specific national policy change using Victorian and national data. Must include an executive summary, three numbered recommendations, evidence base, and anticipated impact metrics. Cite a minimum of five primary sources.
Option B★ — Longitudinal Campaign Strategy
Design a multi-phase, multi-channel water safety campaign targeting CALD males aged 18–35 in Victoria. The campaign must: be grounded in specific LSV and RLSSA data; address structural barriers (not just awareness); include three campaign phases across 12 months; specify platforms, messages, and culturally safe delivery approaches; include an evaluation framework with SMART success metrics; and acknowledge limitations.
Option C★ — Comparative Systems Analysis
Conduct a full comparative analysis of water safety education systems in Australia, the UK, and New Zealand (or another comparable country). Evaluate each against four criteria: curriculum integration, community reach, equity focus, and measurable impact. Conclude with specific recommendations for what Victoria should adopt, adapt, or reject from international approaches. Cite primary sources from all three countries.
These are A-standard tasks requiring 3–5 primary sources, sophisticated argument structure, and evidence of original research beyond the workbook.
