Physiology of Drowning — What Happens in the Body

Key Knowledge — Physiological Processes

  • Hypoxia: Oxygen deprivation. Brain cells begin dying within 4–6 minutes of oxygen loss. After 10 minutes without CPR, the chance of survival without significant brain damage is very low.
  • Cold water shock (1-10-1 rule): ~1 min to control breathing; ~10 min of useful swimming; up to 1 hr before hypothermia incapacitates (varies greatly by water temperature, body composition, and clothing).
  • Wet vs dry drowning: In most drowning cases (wet), water enters the lungs. In ~10–15% (dry), laryngospasm prevents water entry but still causes asphyxia.
  • Secondary drowning: Rare but real — symptoms (coughing, fatigue, chest pain) can appear hours after a near-drowning event. Always seek medical review after a submersion incident.
  • CPR physiology: Chest compressions create approximately 25–30% of normal cardiac output, delivering enough oxygenated blood to keep the brain alive until the heart can restart (spontaneously or via defibrillation).

Sources: St John Ambulance Vic — stjohnvic.com.au · Heart Foundation — heartfoundation.org.au · WHO Drowning Factsheet — who.int

Social Determinants of Drowning Risk

Key Knowledge — Who Is at Risk and Why

  • Social determinants are the non-medical factors that influence health outcomes
  • For drowning: gender norms, cultural background, socioeconomic status, and geography all play a role
  • CALD communities may have limited swimming opportunity due to cultural norms, cost, or country-of-origin practices
  • Masculinity norms ("don't back down from dares") are a documented risk factor for young male drowning
  • Rural and regional Victorians swim at unpatrolled inland waterways at higher rates than urban residents
  • People with lower socioeconomic status are less likely to have learned to swim as children
  • Alcohol is a factor in approximately 20–30% of adult drowning deaths in Victoria

Sources: LSV Drowning Report 2023–24 · RLSSA National Drowning Report · WHO Social Determinants of Health Framework

Evaluating Drowning Prevention Strategies

Key Knowledge — Prevention Approaches and Evidence

  • Engineering: Pool fencing (4-sided isolation) — strongest evidence base of any single intervention. Reduces child drowning in home pools by ~83%.
  • Supervision: Lifeguard presence at patrolled beaches — all 54 Victorian 2023–24 drowning deaths were at unpatrolled locations.
  • Education: Swim lessons — strong evidence for reducing child drowning. Adults benefit less unless combined with behaviour change.
  • Legislation: Pool fencing laws, lifejacket requirements, alcohol restrictions near water.
  • Technology: AEDs, throw rescue equipment, CCTV at public pools, drowning detection systems.
  • Community programs: LSV SwimSafe, RLSSA Swim and Survive, targeted CALD programs.

Sources: WHO Drowning Prevention Guide — who.int · LSV Toolkit — lsv.com.au/toolkit · RLSSA — royallifesaving.com.au

Diving, Spinal Injuries & High-Risk Teenage Behaviour

Key Knowledge — Australian Statistics & Research Context

Diving and jumping into unknown or shallow water is one of the leading causes of catastrophic, permanent injury among Australian teenagers — yet it remains heavily under-discussed in water safety education. Sources: Royal Life Saving Australia (royallifesaving.com.au) · AIHW Diving Injury Report (aihw.gov.au) · Royal Perth Hospital spinal injury study

Highest-Risk Age Groups & Locations

The pattern of injury is not random — it is concentrated in specific age groups, behaviours, and locations. Understanding this pattern is essential for designing effective prevention.

Highest-risk age groups:

  • Children aged 10–14 — highest incidence of diving-related spinal injuries
  • 15–20 year olds — 34% increase in drowning post-COVID
  • Males under 30 — more than 75% of all aquatic spinal injuries

High-risk locations:

  • River bridges and wharves / jetties
  • Dam walls and irrigation channels
  • Rope swings over rivers and quarries
  • Rock platforms and waterfalls
  • Beaches with tidal sandbanks
  • Floodways and drainage channels

Royal Life Saving Australia identifies peer pressure, impulsive behaviour, alcohol, "showing off," jumping from structures, and overconfidence in swimming ability as the major behavioural contributors to teenage aquatic injuries and fatalities.

Australian Case Studies — Recent Incidents

Case Study 1 · NSW 2025

Terrigal Wharf — spinal injury

A teenage boy suffered serious spinal injuries after diving from a wharf at Terrigal into water he believed was deep. Helicopter evacuation to hospital. He was unaware of the reduced water depth beneath the wharf.

Key themes:

  • Unknown depth beneath structures
  • "It looked deep enough" misconception
  • Hidden shallow zones under wharves
  • Catastrophic neck injury from one dive
Case Study 2 · Victoria 2025

Torquay Front Beach — surf beach diving

A teenage boy suffered a serious suspected spinal injury after a diving incident at Torquay Front Beach and was airlifted to hospital. Surf beach sandbanks shift constantly, making depth at any given point highly unpredictable.

Key themes:

  • Sandbanks change with every tide
  • Holiday risk-taking behaviour
  • Lifelong consequences of one dive
  • Surf beaches are not "safe" for diving
Case Study 3 · NSW 2024

Bawley Beach jetty — sandbank formation

A young man suffered serious injuries diving from a jetty near Bawley Beach. A sandbank had recently formed beneath the jumping area, dramatically reducing water depth. Prior experience at the location created false confidence.

Key themes:

  • Depth changes — even at familiar sites
  • "I've done it before" false confidence
  • Tidal sediment movement
  • Local knowledge ≠ current safety

Why Inland Waterways Are Australia's Highest-Risk Environment

Royal Life Saving Australia states: "More people drown in inland waterways than any other location in Australia." Five specific factors explain this:

1. Depth changes constantly

Drought, flooding, irrigation releases, sediment build-up, sandbank movement, and dam level changes all alter depth unpredictably — even between morning and afternoon.

2. Visibility is poor

Rocks, submerged trees, debris, logs, shopping trolleys, and submerged branches are frequently invisible beneath turbid (murky) water.

3. Cold water shock

Many Australian rivers and dams remain cold even in hot weather, triggering panic, muscle cramping, loss of coordination, and drowning within minutes.

4. Alcohol and group risk-taking

Inland water incidents frequently involve alcohol, teenage males, group peer pressure, and jumping from bridges or riverbanks — a combination that significantly multiplies risk.

5. Remote locations delay rescue

River and dam locations often mean delayed ambulance access, limited mobile phone reception, no lifeguards, and delayed spinal immobilisation — all of which worsen outcomes.

The core prevention message

"Feet first, first time." Never dive into unknown water. Always check depth every single time — not just the first time. Previous safety at a location does not guarantee current safety.
