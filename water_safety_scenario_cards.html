🏖 BEACH SCENARIOS
🏖

The Unpatrolled Beach

B1
Scene: It's a hot Saturday in January. Your group of friends (8 of you) arrive at a remote beach near Torquay. There are no flags, no lifeguards, and no signs. The surf looks rough. One friend says, "Don't be a wimp — let's just go in!" Two others start walking toward the water.
📝 Discuss & Answer
  • What are THREE safety concerns with this situation?
  • What would you say to your friends? Write the exact words.
  • What should you do instead of swimming at this location?
  • Why is peer pressure especially dangerous near water?
🔑 Teacher Answers

1. No lifeguards/supervision; rough surf suggests rip current risk; no way to call for help quickly. 2. Accept any assertive response e.g. "I'm not going in — let's find a patrolled beach, it's worth the drive." 3. Use BeachSafe app to find nearest patrolled beach, or stay on shore. 4. Peer pressure causes people to override their own risk assessment — a key factor in teen drowning.

🏖 Beach 👥 Peer pressure SwimSafe: Read Signs · Swim with a Friend
🌊

Caught in a Rip

B2
Scene: Maya, 13, is swimming between the flags at a patrolled beach in Phillip Island. She feels herself being pulled sideways and then out from shore. The more she tries to swim back, the more tired she gets. She's about 30m from shore and starting to panic.
📝 Discuss & Answer
  • What is Maya most likely caught in? How can she tell?
  • What is the WORST thing Maya can do right now? Why?
  • Describe the three steps Maya should take to survive.
  • What should her friends on the beach do?
🔑 Teacher Answers

1. A rip current — she's being pulled outward and sideways despite swimming hard. 2. Continue sprinting directly against the current — it will exhaust her. 3. Stay calm, float/conserve energy, swim parallel to shore to escape the narrow current channel, then angle back. 4. Wave and shout to attract lifeguard attention; do NOT enter the water themselves.

🌊 Rip current 🏊 Survival swimming Signal for Help · Stay Calm · Swim Parallel
🚩

The Flag Problem

B3
Scene: You arrive at Torquay beach with your family. You can see: two red-and-yellow flags about 100m to your left, a black-and-white quartered flag area 30m in front of you, and an orange windsock flying near the patrol tent. Your younger cousin (8 years old) wants to jump in right where you're standing and has brought a blow-up crocodile.
📝 Discuss & Answer
  • What does each of the three flags mean?
  • Where should your family swim? Explain your reasoning.
  • Why is the inflatable crocodile a problem today? Be specific.
  • What would you say to your 8-year-old cousin?
🔑 Teacher Answers

1. Red/yellow = patrolled swimming zone; black/white = watercraft/surfboard zone; orange windsock = offshore winds present. 2. Between the red and yellow flags — supervised and safe. 3. The orange windsock means offshore winds. Inflatables will be carried out to sea. If the cousin falls off in deep water, the inflatable is gone and they're stranded. 4. "We need to walk to the flags first, and you can't bring the crocodile today because the wind will blow it out to sea — it's not safe."

🏖 Beach flags 🟠 Windsock SwimSafe: Read Signs · Swim Between Flags
🏞 RIVER & INLAND WATERWAY SCENARIOS
🏞

The Rope Swing

R1
Scene: A popular river spot in the Yarra Valley has a rope swing over the water. It's a hot day and kids have been using it all afternoon. A Year 9 student you don't know swings out and lets go awkwardly. He hits the water and doesn't come back up. His friends are panicking. You are standing on the bank with a towel and your phone.
📝 Discuss & Answer
  • What is your FIRST action? Why not jump in?
  • How do you use the towel to help without entering the water?
  • Walk through the DRS ABCD steps if the person is pulled out unconscious.
  • What are THREE hazards at this location you should have noticed BEFORE this happened?
🔑 Teacher Answers

1. Call 000, then attempt a throw rescue — jumping in risks a second drowning. 2. Throw one end of the towel to the person; pull them toward the bank. 3. D-Check for danger (rocks, current), R-Tap shoulder/shout, S-Ensure 000 called, A-Open airway, B-Check breathing 10 sec, C-30 compressions + 2 breaths, D-Use AED if available. 4. No lifeguard supervision; unknown water depth; submerged rocks or debris under water at landing point; cold water shock risk.

🏞 River 🩺 DRS ABCD THROW, DON'T GO
🌧

After the Rain

R2
Scene: It rained heavily last night but today is sunny and warm. Your group wants to swim in their favourite river spot near Warburton. The water looks higher and faster than usual, and it's a murky brown colour. One friend says it looks fine and starts getting changed.
📝 Discuss & Answer
  • List FOUR specific hazards that high, murky, fast river water creates.
  • Why does the murky brown colour matter for safety?
  • Your friend says "I swam here last week and it was fine." How do you respond?
  • What is a safer alternative for cooling off today?
🔑 Teacher Answers

1. Stronger current, submerged debris (logs, rocks, fence posts), cold water from mountain runoff, loss of reference points for depth. 2. Murky water = you can't see the bottom or any obstacles. Submerged logs/fences could trap you. 3. "Conditions change completely after rain — what was safe last week could kill you today. The current is completely different." 4. Local public pool; wait 48–72 hours for water to clear and level to drop; go to a patrolled beach.

🌧 Inland waterway ⚠️ Flood conditions Conditions change rapidly
🌡

Cold Water Shock

R3
Scene: Jake (Year 8) and his family are camping near Lake Eildon in December. It's 36°C. Jake sees people on the news about cold water shock but thinks "it's summer so the water must be warm." He runs and jumps into the lake. Within seconds he gasps involuntarily, his muscles seize up, and he can't control his breathing. He's only in waist-deep water but starts to panic.
📝 Discuss & Answer
  • What is cold water shock and why did it happen on a hot day?
  • What should Jake's parents have told him before he got in?
  • Why is the "gasping" reflex so dangerous even in shallow water?
  • What is the safe way to enter cold water?
🔑 Teacher Answers

1. Involuntary body response when skin temperature drops suddenly. Alpine lakes like Eildon are fed by snowmelt and can be 12–15°C even in summer regardless of air temperature. 2. Enter slowly, check the temperature, don't jump in, stay shallow. 3. If you gasp and inhale water you can drown even in waist-deep water before you can stand up. 4. Wade in gradually, acclimatise, never jump into unknown water, always enter feet first.

🌡 Cold water 🏔 Alpine lakes Enter feet first · Acclimatise slowly
🏊 POOL & BACKYARD SCENARIOS
🏊

Pool Party Emergency

P1
Scene: You're at a friend's pool party (Year 8, about 20 kids). The adults have gone inside to get food. Everyone is laughing and music is loud. You look over and notice a 13-year-old face-down and motionless at the shallow end. No one else has noticed.
📝 Discuss & Answer
  • Describe exactly what you do in the first 30 seconds.
  • Write the words you would shout to get adult help.
  • If the person is not breathing, what do you do? List the steps in order.
  • What could have been done BEFORE this happened to reduce the risk?
🔑 Teacher Answers

1. Shout for help immediately, pull person to the edge using pool noodle/your arm from the edge if possible (don't jump in), check for response. 2. "CALL 000 NOW — SOMEONE IS DROWNING IN THE POOL" — use direct, clear language. 3. Place on flat surface, D-check for danger, check response, send for help (000), open airway, check breathing 10 sec, begin CPR (30:2). 4. Designated adult water watcher; no distraction (music/phones reduced); buddy system; everyone know where towrope/rescue equipment is.

🏊 Pool 🩺 DRS ABCD Act immediately · Call 000
🏠

The Backyard Pool

P2
Scene: You're babysitting your 4-year-old neighbour at their home. The backyard has a pool. The gate appears to be shut. You're inside making lunch and you hear silence — you realise you haven't heard the child in a few minutes.
📝 Discuss & Answer
  • What do you do immediately?
  • Why is "silence" actually a warning sign around water with young children?
  • What should have been done when you first arrived as the babysitter?
  • What are THREE pool safety features every backyard pool should have?
🔑 Teacher Answers

1. Check the backyard immediately — if child is in pool, begin DRS ABCD, call 000. 2. Drowning is silent — there's no splashing or calling for help. Children slip under quietly. A silent child near a pool means check immediately. 3. Check all pool gates are properly latched (4-sided fencing); know where the child is at ALL times; check the pool area. 4. 4-sided isolation fencing (1.2m+), self-closing/latching gate, no furniture near fence to climb over, CPR chart displayed.

🏠 Backyard pool 👧 Young child safety Drowning is silent · Active supervision
🩺 EMERGENCY RESPONSE SCENARIOS
🆘

Calling 000

E1
Scene: You are at an unpatrolled beach. An adult has been pulled out of the water by two bystanders. He is lying on the sand, unconscious, and not breathing. One person is attempting CPR. Your job is to call 000. You're shaking.
📝 Discuss & Answer
  • Write down exactly what you would say when 000 answers.
  • What information does the operator NEED from you?
  • Should you hang up once you've given the information? Why or why not?
  • What if you don't know the exact address?
🔑 Teacher Answers

1. Sample: "Ambulance please. I'm at [beach name] near [nearest suburb/landmark]. A man has been pulled from the water. He is unconscious and not breathing. CPR is in progress." 2. Location, what happened, number of people injured, whether they are breathing, your name and number. 3. Never hang up — stay on the line, the operator will guide CPR and send help. 4. Describe the nearest landmark, carpark, road name — or use the what3words app location. Give the best description you can.

📞 000 🚑 Emergency services Never hang up · Give exact location
🪢

Throw Rescue

E2
Scene: A young woman has slipped off the jetty at a lake near Gippsland and is struggling in the water 5 metres from the edge. She can't swim. You have: a coiled rope, a life ring attached to the jetty, your jumper, and a wooden plank on the jetty. She is panicking and going under.
📝 Discuss & Answer
  • Rank the four items from BEST to WORST rescue tool. Explain your ranking.
  • Describe HOW you would use the best rescue tool. Be specific.
  • Why should you NOT jump in to save her, even if you're a strong swimmer?
  • What should you shout to her while you're attempting the rescue?
🔑 Teacher Answers

1. Best: Life ring (attached, won't let go) → Rope (throw and pull) → Plank (she can grab on) → Jumper (limited reach). 2. Unclip the life ring, shout "Grab this!", throw it slightly past her so she can grab it as it drifts back, then pull steadily while keeping low. 3. A panicking person will climb on you and push you under — even strong swimmers have drowned this way. Stay on the jetty. 4. "STAY CALM! I'M THROWING YOU A RING! GRAB IT AND HOLD ON!"

🪢 Throw rescue 🚫 Don't enter water Throw, Don't Go

The Group Decision

D1
Scene: It's New Year's Day. You're with 10 friends at the Mornington Peninsula. The patrolled beach is a 20-minute walk away. Half the group wants to swim at the nearby unpatrolled beach "to save time." The other half (including you) wants to go to the patrolled beach. The unpatrolled beach has no signs, no flags, and looks rougher.
📝 Discuss & Answer
  • Using the LSV drowning data: what time of year and location are drownings most likely?
  • Write a persuasive argument to convince the group to walk to the patrolled beach.
  • What does "All 54 Victorian drowning deaths in 2023–24 happened at unpatrolled locations" tell your friends?
  • If you're outvoted and the group swims at the unpatrolled beach, what precautions should you all take?
🔑 Teacher Answers

1. Summer (Dec-Feb) at unpatrolled beaches, rivers, and dams. 2. Accept compelling arguments using statistics and specific risk factors. 3. It tells them that NO ONE has drowned at a patrolled beach in Victoria in the entire year — the data proves patrolled beaches save lives. 4. Never swim alone, stay shallow, swim with a buddy, know where everyone is, have someone watch from shore, know how to call 000.

⚖️ Decision-making 📊 Uses LSV data Always swim between the flags

Alcohol & Water

D2
Scene: A group of 18-year-olds are celebrating finishing Year 12. They're at a house with a pool. Most have been drinking. One person (who has had several drinks) decides to jump in the pool for a swim at 1am to "cool off." His friends cheer him on.
📝 Discuss & Answer
  • List FOUR ways alcohol specifically increases drowning risk.
  • What should his friends do? What would most people actually do? Why is there a gap?
  • Why is night-time swimming especially risky?
  • If someone in this situation becomes unconscious in the pool, what complicates the DRS ABCD response?
🔑 Teacher Answers

1. Impaired judgement (overestimates ability), impaired coordination, slowed reaction time, impaired body temperature regulation (hypothermia risk). 2. Should: stop him, suggest getting out, stay with him. Most would: cheer because peer pressure and alcohol reduce risk perception. 3. Poor visibility = harder to see if someone goes under; harder to see hazards; slower response. 4. In water CPR is ineffective — must get person out first, which takes time; alcohol in the blood complicates assessment of consciousness level.

🍺 Alcohol risk 🌙 Night swimming Never mix alcohol and water
