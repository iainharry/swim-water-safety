Acknowledgement of Country

We acknowledge the Traditional Custodians of the lands and waters on which we teach and learn. We pay our respects to Elders past, present, and emerging. We recognise that waterways — rivers, lakes, estuaries, coastlines, and seas — hold deep cultural, spiritual, and practical significance for First Nations peoples across Australia. This significance predates European settlement by tens of thousands of years, and continues today.

In teaching water safety, we acknowledge that we are working within landscapes that are not simply natural features — they are Country. We teach on, near, and about waters that carry stories, protocols, and relationships that we must approach with care and respect.

🌊

Why Cultural Considerations Matter in Water Safety Education

🌿 First Nations Relationships with Water

For Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples, waterways are not simply recreational spaces or safety hazards — they are places of deep cultural, spiritual, and ecological significance. Rivers, billabongs, coastlines, and wetlands are embedded in Songlines, Dreamtime stories, and custodianship responsibilities that have been maintained for over 60,000 years.

Water safety education that treats waterways purely as physical environments to be navigated or avoided may inadvertently fail to acknowledge this deeper relationship. Respectful teaching acknowledges that Country — including water — is alive with meaning and that First Nations peoples have sophisticated, place-based knowledge about water that predates Western safety frameworks.

When teaching about safe and unsafe waterways, be aware that for some students and families, particular waterways carry cultural significance that shapes how they engage with those places. This is not a barrier to safety — it is context that enriches safety education.

🌀 Cultural Protocols Around Water

Different Aboriginal communities across Australia have different protocols related to water. While these vary significantly between Country and community, some general principles that teachers should be aware of include:

  • Some waterways are sacred and entry may be restricted for cultural reasons. This is a legitimate and important form of water management that deserves respect, not dismissal.
  • Some communities have cultural protocols about discussing certain waterways, particularly those associated with significant ceremonies or Dreaming stories. If a student is reluctant to engage with content about a specific waterway, this may reflect cultural respect rather than disengagement.
  • In many communities, water is associated with spiritual beings and presences. This should be acknowledged respectfully — not dismissed as superstition — as it is a legitimate cultural worldview.
  • Fishing and gathering from waterways is a cultural practice with deep significance beyond recreation. Water safety in these contexts carries different meaning and different stakes for some community members.

Teachers do not need to be experts in the specific protocols of every community. The key is to approach the subject with humility, curiosity, and a willingness to learn from students and families.

⚠️ First Nations Over-Representation in Drowning Data

Australian drowning statistics show that Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples experience significantly higher drowning rates than non-Indigenous Australians. This is a structural and historical inequity, not a reflection on individual behaviour or communities. Contributing factors include:

  • Historical exclusion from public swimming pools under segregation-era policies — creating generational skill gaps that persist today
  • Geographic factors — higher rates of rural and remote living near unpatrolled waterways without lifeguard infrastructure
  • Limited access to swimming lessons and aquatic education in remote communities
  • Language barriers to mainstream water safety messaging
  • Higher rates of occupational waterway exposure (fishing, hunting) without access to modern safety equipment

When teaching about drowning disproportionality in Year 9–10, always frame First Nations over-representation as a structural inequity requiring structural solutions — not as a cultural or behavioural deficit. This framing is both accurate and respectful.

Culturally Responsive Classroom Practice

📋 What to Do — and What to Avoid

Context Recommended approach Avoid
Discussing Victorian drowning statistics that show First Nations over-representation Frame as structural inequity. Acknowledge historical exclusion from swim facilities. Connect to community-level solutions. Framing as individual or cultural failing. Implying that Indigenous communities "don't value" swimming or safety.
Teaching about dangerous waterways (rivers, billabongs, remote beaches) Acknowledge that these are also sites of cultural significance. Teach safety without dismissing the value of the place. Describing waterways only as hazards. Ignoring that some students' families have deep, legitimate relationships with these places.
Discussing the BeachSafe app and lifeguard patrols Acknowledge that lifeguard infrastructure is not evenly distributed and that remote communities have fewer resources. Implying that people who swim at unpatrolled locations are simply making bad choices.
Opening a lesson near a significant waterway (e.g. Yarra River, Port Phillip Bay, Murray) Name the Traditional Custodians of that waterway. Acknowledge its cultural significance before discussing safety. Treating the waterway purely as a physical environment with no cultural dimensions.
Engaging Aboriginal or Torres Strait Islander students in the class Invite (not demand) sharing of community knowledge. Treat shared cultural knowledge with respect and as valuable. Putting students on the spot as "representatives" of all First Nations peoples. Over-focusing on trauma or deficit narratives.
CALD students and families from communities with limited swim access Frame limited swim access as a structural issue. Provide information about free and low-cost community programs. Implying that families who haven't prioritised swimming lessons are being negligent.

🌱 Integrating First Nations Knowledge — Classroom Protocols

🗣️ Invitation, not obligation

Invite First Nations students to share knowledge about their Country's waterways — but never require them to do so. Sharing cultural knowledge is a choice, not a curriculum responsibility.

📍 Localise the Acknowledgement

Name the specific Country and Custodians for your local waterways — not just a generic acknowledgement. AIATSIS mapping resources can help identify Traditional Custodians at aiatsis.gov.au/explore/map-indigenous-australia.

📚 Use verified resources

When incorporating First Nations water knowledge, use resources developed in collaboration with community — not third-party summaries. AIATSIS and local Land Councils are reliable starting points.

🔄 Reciprocal learning

Frame the unit as: "Western safety frameworks have important knowledge — so do First Nations custodianship traditions. We can learn from both." This positions Indigenous knowledge as complementary, not subordinate.

🏫 Connect with community

If your school is in or near a community with strong First Nations connections, consider inviting an Elder or community member to speak about their relationship with local waterways.

CALD Communities — Culturally Responsive Practice

🤝 Approaching CALD Drowning Risk with Respect

CALD communities are over-represented in Victorian drowning deaths at approximately 1.43 times the rate expected based on population share. This is a structural inequity — driven by historical limited swim access, language barriers to safety messaging, and occupational risk factors — not a cultural deficit.

  • Avoid deficit framing: Never imply that CALD families "don't care about safety" or "choose not to swim." Limited swim access is structural, not attitudinal.
  • Multilingual resources: The suite includes a multilingual parent information sheet (English, Simplified Chinese, Hindi, Burmese). Use it. Share it with families.
  • Mixed-gender facilities: Be aware that some students and families may have cultural concerns about mixed-gender swimming environments. Acknowledge this respectfully and work with families where possible.
  • Religious and cultural dress: Modest swimwear is legitimate and should be accommodated. Never frame cultural dress as a safety barrier in ways that are shaming.
  • Community programs: Life Saving Victoria's multicultural safety team runs targeted swim programs for CALD communities. Families can be referred to lsv.com.au/community/multicultural-safety/

Reflection Prompts for Teachers

Does my teaching about dangerous waterways acknowledge that these places also hold cultural significance for some students and their families?
When I discuss drowning statistics, do I explain structural causes — or does my framing inadvertently suggest individual or cultural fault?
Do I know the Traditional Custodians of the waterways most relevant to my students' lives — the local beach, river, or reservoir?
Are my resources accessible to EAL/D families? Have I shared the multilingual parent information sheet?
When a First Nations student shares knowledge about Country, do I treat it with the same respect I would give to a citation from a scientific source?
Do I have connections to local First Nations community members or organisations who could enrich this unit?
